On this data, I trained an XGBoost model with 100 estimators and a maximum depth of 3. Before training, I divided the set into training and test sets to then evaluate the model. I obtained an accuracy of 0.8723 and an AUC of 0.9318.

I chose two observations, the first positive and the second negative, to see how the model would behave. Below I show the observations I selected.

The model classified both cases well, for the first I got a prediction of 0.816 and for the second I got a prediction of 0.0006. To see where these predictions came from, I used the LIME decomposition.

For the first prediction, moderate values of fLength and fWidth were crucial. The negative classification of the second was determined by high values of fAlpha and fWidth.

To better analyse the model, I selected 5 observations that should be classified as gamma radiation and compared the results.

Of these 5 observations, one was classified incorrectly. The model got the second one wrong. Thanks to the LIME decomposition, we can see that this was due to the high values of fAlpha and fWidth. We can also see that 4 of the 5 selected observations have high fAsym values. This factor, however, has a negative impact on the prediction, so in their cases the prediction was lowered.

I also trained a second model, AdaBoost, on the same dataset. I performed its evaluation in an analogous manner and obtained an accuracy of 0.8391 and an AUC of 0.8944. I compared the predictive explanations of the two models for the two observations I mentioned above.

We can see some inaccuracies here. For the first observation, let us pay attention to the feature fDist. In the XGBoost model it had a negative impact on the prediction, while in the AdaBoost model it was already clearly positive. We can observe a similar situation for the second observation and the variable fM3Trans. It had a positive effect in one model and a negative effect in the other.
